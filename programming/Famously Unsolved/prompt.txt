Famed mathemetician Dr. Lothar Collatz had many lasting effects on math, however, his most well-known idea was what became known as the Collatz Conjecture, a problem left unsolved to this day. The conjecture is as follows: start by taking any positive, non-zero integer. If the integer is even, divide it by two; if it is odd, multiply it by three and add one. Take the result and repeat these steps, and after enough repetitions, all possible inputs will eventually narrow down to the loop of 4, 2, 1, with 1 jumping back up to 4 again. While it has been proven for increasingly higher numbers - past two sextillion, in fact - no one has yet been able to prove that it is true for all numbers.

That being said, you can still utilize it today. Your job is to calculate the number of operations any given positive integer will need in the Collatz format before it reaches 1, thus starting the loop. For example, if an input of 4 was given, it would be halved once to give 2, then again to give 1, needing 2 operations. Recursion may help you here, although you aren't strictly required to use it, if you can find a way around it. You may presume that the input will only ever be positive, non-zero integers. Good luck :)
